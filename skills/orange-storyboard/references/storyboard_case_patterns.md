# Storyboard Case Patterns

This reference distills the recurring structures from the source storyboard examples. Use it to choose a pattern before writing the final Image2 prompt.

## Universal Formula

Most prompts should follow this order:

1. Opening task line: `创建一张专业电影级九宫格故事板，用于 [项目]《[标题]》的前期制作。`
2. Layout lock: aspect ratio, 3x3 grid, exactly nine panels, clean borders, left-to-right/top-to-bottom reading order.
3. Core idea: what changes emotionally, commercially, or visually.
4. Character/product/prop locks.
5. Scene lock: where the panels happen and which panels may change location.
6. Style lock: concrete camera, lighting, color, rendering, and texture.
7. Nine panels with titled beats.
8. Negative constraints that protect continuity and prevent unwanted text, logos, UI, extra characters, style drift, or poster collage.

## Emotional Anime Short

Best for restrained romantic, coming-of-age, nostalgic, or bittersweet shorts.

Typical rhythm:

1. Establish the place and emotional distance.
2. Show the characters together in a quiet shared moment.
3. Detail close-up of the tiny action that carries the emotion.
4. The action almost happens.
5. Interruption or missed timing.
6. Object/body detail that holds the emotional weight.
7. One character leaves or turns away.
8. The remaining character absorbs the moment.
9. Wide ending with landscape and emotional aftertaste.

Rules:

- Use tiny physical actions instead of explicit dialogue.
- Repeat hand, object, wind, or light motifs.
- Keep facial expressions restrained; emotion lives in eyes, posture, distance, and stillness.
- Good terms: `anime film aesthetic`, `delicate hand-painted texture`, `golden sunset backlight`, `atmospheric perspective`, `quiet emotional restraint`.

## Before-Story / Prequel Board

Use when the user asks for "发生之前", first chapter, or setup before a known storyboard.

Typical rhythm:

1. Energetic opening action.
2. Physical detail that introduces pace.
3. Small accident or unstable moment.
4. First meaningful contact.
5. Casual response from the unaware character.
6. Character continues naturally.
7. Emotional residue detail.
8. Protagonist reacts and follows.
9. End frame connects directly to the next storyboard's first frame.

Always state the final panel must visually match or lead into the next storyboard.

## Luxury Product TVC

Best for watches, perfume, jewelry, cars, cosmetics, alcohol, fashion, and premium tech.

Typical rhythm:

1. World-level metaphor or atmosphere.
2. Detail begins to move.
3. Many elements gather with order.
4. Dynamic macro or tracking shot.
5. Abstracted but still concrete formation.
6. `match cut` into the real product reveal.
7. Macro craftsmanship or material proof.
8. Lifestyle or use moment.
9. Final `hero shot` with space for copy but no actual text.

Rules:

- Lock the product's shape, material, color, and logo/text policy before the panels.
- The product should usually first appear fully around panel 6, then become increasingly clear.
- Use visible premium details: metal reflection, glass refraction, leather grain, liquid, mist, petals, precision parts.
- Avoid "廉价电商图", real brand names, fake logos, readable text, random extra products, and overstuffed backgrounds.

## Action Comedy / 3D Animation Short

Best for cute chase, delivery, magical object, market, town, or family-friendly concepts.

Typical rhythm:

1. Receive or discover the mission object.
2. Rush into a lively public space.
3. First obstacle.
4. Spatial squeeze or near miss.
5. Bigger kinetic obstacle.
6. Object almost lost.
7. Sudden pause or public reveal setup.
8. The object opens or reveals meaning.
9. Warm group payoff.

Rules:

- Make spatial relationships readable; comedy fails if the action is visually confusing.
- Keep danger playful, not harmful.
- Lock the hero outfit and mission object in every panel.
- Keep reveal objects hidden until the intended panel.

## Travel / Culture Promo

Best for tourism, city promo, regional culture, dance-led transitions, or scenic montage.

Typical rhythm:

1. Start with a clear cultural gesture or protagonist pose.
2. Introduce the first landmark through action.
3. Use movement to transform into a second location.
4. Landmark and body line echo each other.
5. Smaller local detail.
6. Grand scenic peak.
7. Return to a recognizable local symbol.
8. Next destination appears.
9. Transition element prepares the next storyboard.

Rules:

- One main place, one main action, and one transition element per panel.
- Lock palette tightly if the concept depends on a graphic style.
- Preserve breathing space; do not turn tourism boards into crowded postcards.

## Wuxia Encounter

Best for classical martial arts, restrained first meeting, sword, bamboo, guqin, mountains, and Jianghu atmosphere.

Typical rhythm:

1. Quiet environment establishes qi and space.
2. One character alone.
3. The other character appears at a distance.
4. Wind or object senses the encounter first.
5. First eye contact across space.
6. Eye or object close-up.
7. They turn away without speaking.
8. A physical detail keeps the emotional trace.
9. Wide ending returns to wind, bamboo, path, instrument, or sword.

Rules:

- Prefer stillness, negative space, long-lens compression, and controlled movement.
- Avoid modern xianxia effects, glowing magic, crowded fights, game armor, and exaggerated emotions.
- Good terms: `locked camera`, `slow dolly`, `rack focus`, `film grain`, `low-saturation green`, `classical wuxia cinema`.

## Cyberpunk Memory / Noir

Best for neon rain, old machines, memory, loneliness, future nostalgia, and restrained sci-fi.

Typical rhythm:

1. Neon environment and isolated protagonist.
2. Strange object or machine discovered.
3. Ritual interaction such as inserting a coin.
4. Future object appears.
5. Projection or memory reveal.
6. Memory light changes the protagonist.
7. Choice not to consume/use the object.
8. Object is returned or sealed.
9. Protagonist disappears back into the environment.

Rules:

- Use old physical texture plus future glow as the central contrast.
- Keep the sci-fi object specific and readable.
- Avoid overdecorated neon, game UI, crowds, readable text, and making the protagonist perform the opposite of the story choice.

## Character Board

Use only when the user asks for a role design sheet, not a story sequence.

Include:

- Full-body front-facing hero pose.
- 6-9 action or expression variants.
- Costume, weapon, props, silhouette, face, hairstyle, color palette.
- Clear constraints for identity consistency.

Do not force the nine-panel story rhythm onto a character design board.
